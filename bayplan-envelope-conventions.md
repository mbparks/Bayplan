# BAYPLAN (FI-075): ENVELOPE Conventions and Built-in Library

Companion to `bayplan-data-model.md` (section 6, ClearanceZone) and `bayplan-seed-library.json`.
This document pins the default clearance depths per machine category, closing the ENVELOPE open item from section 13 of the data-model spec.

Document revision: 1.0.0
Schema version: 1.0.0

---

## 1. What this covers

The data-model spec defines *how* a clearance zone is shaped (rect, polygon, arc) and *how* conflicts resolve (hard, soft, shared). It deliberately left the actual default numbers to ENVELOPE. This document supplies them: a local-frame convention every built-in definition follows, a rationale table for the default depths, and per-category notes. The machine-readable output is `bayplan-seed-library.json`, generated by `build_seed.py`.

These are defaults meant to be tuned. The whole point of seed-then-own is that you measure your real planer and nudge it. The numbers below are defensible starting values, not gospel.

## 2. Local frame convention

Every `MachineDef` footprint and clearance is expressed in the machine's own local frame, so it rotates and mirrors with the placement.

| Element | Convention |
|---------|-----------|
| Origin (0,0) | The anchor (blade, spindle, cutterhead, table center) |
| +y | Back, away from the operator |
| -y | Front, the operator side |
| +x | Operator's right |
| -x | Operator's left |
| Units | Inches, floating point |

### Footprint edge names

Built-in footprints are axis-aligned rectangles. A `RectOffEdge` clearance references one of four named edges, and the strip projects outward from that edge:

- **front** = the minimum-y edge (operator side)
- **back** = the maximum-y edge
- **left** = the minimum-x edge
- **right** = the maximum-x edge

The anchor is not always the centroid. A table saw's anchor is the blade, so its footprint is offset (blade 14 inches from the left edge, 13 from the front). The edge names still refer to the footprint's real sides regardless of where the anchor sits inside it.

### Arc angle convention

Arc clearances use degrees clockwise from +y (back), matching the plat's clockwise-from-north scheme with +y as local north:

- 0 = back (+y)
- 90 = right (+x)
- 180 = front (-y)
- 270 = left (-x)

The wood lathe's outboard swing, for example, is an arc centered at the headstock spindle sweeping from 180 to 270 (front to left), the quadrant where an outboard bowl blank rotates.

### minHeight

`minHeight` is the floor height of the underside of a zone's protected band. The zone requires clearance from `minHeight` up to the ceiling; a value of `null` means the band starts at the floor (full height). So an obstruction shorter than a zone's `minHeight` passes harmlessly under it, while anything taller intrudes. This is what lets a table saw's outfeed lane pass over a short storage unit (its `minHeight` sits near deck height) while an operator stance, with a `null` `minHeight`, is protected floor to ceiling and tolerates nothing beneath it. A machine body occupies the band from the floor to its own `height`, so TRIM flags a body-in-zone conflict only when the body is taller than the zone's `minHeight`.

## 3. Default clearance depths (the rationale table)

| Zone kind | Default depth | Severity | minHeight | Reasoning |
|-----------|--------------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Feed lane, sheet-goods capable | 96 | shared | deck + stock | 8 feet each side to rip or crosscut a full sheet |
| Feed lane, long stock | 72 to 96 | shared | deck + stock | Matched to typical board and trim lengths |
| Operator standing | 30 (36 comfortable) | soft | null | Room to stand and work; full height |
| Operator seated | 30 | soft | null | Chair plus reach at a bench |
| Service access | 18 to 24 | soft | null | Panels, bins, bit changes, wiring |
| Spark and fire standoff | 36 to 48 | hard | null | Welding slag and grinder spark cones |
| Immediate blade danger | 8 | hard | null | The kill zone right at the cutter |
| Swing (lathe outboard) | radius + margin | hard | null | Nothing may enter the arc of a spinning blank |
| End clearance (lathe, bar stock) | 18 to 24 | hard or soft | varies | Bar through the spindle bore, long stock past the tailstock |

### Why feed lanes are shared, not hard

A table saw's outfeed genuinely wants to overlap the jointer's operator walk space, or an aisle, or another machine's low base. Marking feed lanes `shared` lets two feed zones (or a feed zone and an aisle) coexist without a false conflict, while a `shared` lane hitting a `hard` zone (someone's blade danger zone, a lathe swing) still errors. The `minHeight` on a feed lane reinforces this in the vertical dimension: the lane's protected band sits above deck height, so a machine body shorter than that height passes under it without a flag.

### Why operator zones are soft

Two operator stances that will not be used at the same time may overlap; that is a warning, not an error. You know your own workflow. The tool flags the overlap so you make the call deliberately rather than discovering it at the bench.

### Why fire, blade, and swing are hard

These are safety zones, not convenience zones. A combustible inside a welding spark spray, a body inside a blade path, or a fixture inside a lathe swing is an error the tool should refuse to pass quietly. Hard is the only correct severity for anything that can hurt you.

### Primitives exercised by the seed

The seed is also a coverage test for the geometry engine. Across 50 clearance zones it exercises every primitive and every severity:

- **rect** on all four named edges, with and without `minHeight`
- **arc** on the wood lathe (the outboard swing), the one primitive hardest to get right under mirroring
- **shared** severity on feed lanes and the assembly apron (15 zones)
- **hard** severity on blade, fire, swing, and bar-stock zones (7 zones)
- **soft** severity on operator and service zones (the balance)

If a rendering or conflict-checking change passes against this seed, it has seen every shape and severity combination the model defines.

## 5. Category defaults not yet in the seed

Categories a future seed pass could add, with the depth family they would inherit:

- Belt or disc sander: operator soft, dust 4in, no long feed lane.
- Router table: feed lanes like a jointer, dust 2.5in at the fence.
- Surface grinder or metal cutoff saw: long feed lanes, coolant or chip service.
- Spray or finishing booth: fire standoff (hard), strong ventilation clearance.
- Sheet-goods rack and lumber rack: no operator zone, a loading apron (shared).

Each would follow the section-3 rationale table rather than inventing new depths.

## 6. SERVICES defaults (duct and electrical)

This section pins the tunable defaults the SERVICES station uses, closing the elbow-equivalent open item from section 13 of the data-model spec. Like the clearance depths, these are defensible starting values, not gospel, and they live in the `Services` module of `bayplan.html`.

### Duct elbow equivalents

A fitting adds resistance equivalent to some length of straight duct. BAYPLAN charges each bend in a run an equivalent length, in feet, for one ninety-degree elbow at that diameter, and scales linearly for shallower bends (a forty-five-degree bend costs half a ninety).

| Duct diameter | 90-degree elbow equivalent |
|---------------|----------------------------|
| 2.5 in | 4 ft |
| 4 in | 6 ft |
| 5 in | 7 ft |
| 6 in | 9 ft |

Elbows are inferred from a run's polyline: at each interior vertex the deflection from straight is measured, and the elbow charge is that table value times (deflection / 90). A straight run has no interior vertices and therefore no elbow charge. Auto-routed dust runs turn one orthogonal corner, so they carry exactly one ninety-degree elbow unless the collector and the machine port already share an axis.

### Duct effective-length budget

Effective length is straight run plus elbow equivalents. A run past the recommended maximum for its diameter earns a warning, on the principle that a long small-diameter run cannot keep chips moving.

| Duct diameter | Recommended max effective length |
|---------------|----------------------------------|
| 2.5 in | 20 ft |
| 4 in | 35 ft |
| 5 in | 45 ft |
| 6 in | 55 ft |

These are rule-of-thumb ceilings for typical single-stage and cyclone collectors in a small shop, deliberately conservative. A precise design would compute static pressure against the collector's fan curve; that refinement is noted for a future pass.

### Electrical rollup

Each machine carries a `PowerReq` (voltage, amps, phase, dedicated). When a panel exists, SERVICES rolls up connected load into a 120V bucket and a 240V bucket. Two checks apply: a machine that needs 240V when no panel supplies it is an error, and connected load past eighty percent of the largest panel's main is a warning. For the load-versus-main comparison, 120V loads are counted at half their amperage, since two 120V circuits share a 240V main. Panel defaults are a 240V service at 100A main with 20 spaces; these are editable per node.

### Per-circuit assignment

Beyond the whole-panel rollup, each electrical run is assigned to a specific circuit (breaker) on its panel. Wire all assigns automatically: a machine flagged `dedicated` gets its own breaker sized to the next standard rating above its load with the eighty-percent continuous-load margin applied, and non-dedicated machines pack into shared breakers of matching voltage while each breaker stays under eighty percent of its rating. Standard breaker sizes are 15, 20, 30, 40, 50, and 60A. A circuit whose connected load exceeds eighty percent of its breaker is a warning, and one that exceeds the breaker outright is an error, as is a run whose voltage is higher than its circuit's. Assignments can be overridden per run in the utilities panel.

### Display units

The model is stored in inches throughout; `meta.units` (`imperial` or `metric`) is a display setting only. The header toggle switches every length shown in the interface, the rulers, the measuring tape, the machine width and depth fields, the room-dimension inputs, and the printed title block, between feet-and-inches and meters-and-centimeters. Because only the display converts and the stored geometry never changes, switching units back and forth is lossless. Engineering figures that are conventionally quoted in their own units regardless of shop preference, duct diameters in inches, airflow in CFM, breaker amperage, and voltage, are left as-is.

### Light fixtures

Light fixtures are a utility node placed on the ceiling plan and specified by wattage (default a 40W 120V LED shop fixture, editable per node). Wire all runs each fixture to the nearest panel and groups them onto shared 15A lighting circuits, kept distinct from receptacle and machine circuits, by connected watts under the eighty percent rule (so dozens of LED fixtures ride one circuit). A fixture's connected load is its watts over its voltage, which feeds the panel rollup and the per-circuit report like any other load, and a fixture placed without a panel is flagged. Fixtures are tallied in the takeoff by wattage, and their circuits and cable fall into the breaker and copper totals with everything else.

### Note visibility

Notes have a plat-wide show/hide toggle in the header. Hidden notes are not drawn and cannot be selected or dragged, and the printed plat and takeoff PDF follow the same toggle, so a drawing can be issued with or without its working annotations. Visibility is a view setting and does not change or delete the notes themselves.

### Tool tags, plat notes, and the saved file

A placed machine can carry a tag, its instance name, so two of the same tool are distinguishable: set it in the selection panel and it shows on the plat, in the on-floor list, and in the takeoff wherever that machine is named. Plat notes are free-text annotations pinned anywhere on the floor: add one from the header, drag its pin, type into it, and it renders where you put it, on screen and on the printed plat. Notes belong to the scenario, so each arrangement keeps its own.

Everything on the plat is part of one document, and that document is what gets saved and restored. Export writes the whole document as JSON (the built-in machine library is the one thing left out, since it is reattached on load), and import reads it back and fills in any arrays an older file predates. Tags, resized footprints, notes, the unit setting, every utility node and run with its bends and circuit assignments: all of it rides through export, import, and autosave together, because all of it lives in the one document. Files are the source of truth; the library is the only shared constant reattached from the build.

### Touch and zoom

The plat is a windowed viewBox: the SVG content is drawn once in full plat coordinates, and a view window (a sub-rectangle of that space) is what the viewBox shows, so zoom and pan are pure changes to the window and never touch the content or the world-to-screen mapping. This is what lets zoom compose cleanly with the drag coordinate fixes rather than fighting them: `toScreen` still returns full-plat coordinates, and only `screenToWorld` inverts the current window. The window tracks the whole plat until the user zooms, then persists; Fit clears that state and returns to full. Zoom clamps between the full plat (most zoomed out) and a fixed fraction of it (most zoomed in), and holds the plat's aspect so the drawing never distorts. Two fingers pinch to zoom and pan around their midpoint; a single finger or the mouse keeps its existing select-and-drag behavior, so the two gestures do not collide. The mouse wheel zooms toward the cursor. Because hit tolerances are expressed in inches of floor, zooming in is also how you get finger-precision on small handles, and tap targets in the chrome grow under a coarse-pointer media query.

### Standalone viewer export

Publish bakes the plan into a single self-contained HTML file for handoff: the plat drawing (rendered with the same print palette as the printed sheet, so it carries literal colors and needs no app stylesheet), a machine schedule counting the placed tools, a severity and system legend, a title block, and a scenario tab bar when there is more than one scenario. The only stylesheet dependency the baked SVG has is the monospace font variable, which the viewer's own `:root` defines, so the file renders correctly anywhere with no external assets, no data, and no editor code. It is deliberately read-only: a drawing to share, distinct from the editable `.bayplan.json` export, which carries the full document (including the user library) for continued work. Because it is a static snapshot, it is regenerated and re-shared after changes rather than staying live.

### Custom machine builder

A custom machine is a MachineDef authored through the builder instead of the seed generator, stored in the document's `userLibrary` so it saves and loads with the plan. The builder keeps to rectangles: a width and depth become a footprint centered on the anchor, and each nonzero edge depth becomes a rectangular clearance projected off that edge, using the same front/back/left/right convention as the seed (front is the -y operator side). Amps and voltage become the power block, marked dedicated at 240V or 30A and above, or null when amps are zero; the dust and air toggles add centered ports; and two work points are placed at the front and back so material flow can snap to it. Provenance is marked `user` (as opposed to `builtin` or a QUARTERMASTER seed), which keeps custom tools distinguishable from the roster. Once saved, a custom machine is indistinguishable to the rest of the tool from a built-in: it places from the shelf, checks its clearances, wires, routes, and prints identically. It is deletable only when no placement references it, matching the archive-over-delete posture, and it is edited by deleting and recreating (the builder does not yet reopen an existing definition). The builder does not author non-rectangular footprints, offset anchors, arc clearances, per-zone minimum heights, or multiple named work points; those remain the seed generator's domain.

### Circulation and egress

The circulation check samples the floor on a coarse grid, marks each cell blocked if it lies outside the room or inside a machine footprint or a column, and runs a distance transform so every open cell knows its distance to the nearest obstacle or wall; twice that distance is the passage width at that point. Reachability is a flood fill: for a given width it seeds just inside each door at the first cell wide enough, then spreads through cells whose passage width clears that width, so the network follows the aisle centerlines. A machine counts as reachable if that network, dilated out to the machine, touches the free cells around its footprint. The check runs this at a walking width (36in) and an adjustable material width (48in default): a machine the walking network cannot reach is an egress error, one reachable on foot but not with material is a warning, and a door narrower than the material width is flagged because a sheet will not pass it. The aisle width serving the shop is the widest network that still reaches every machine. Findings feed the readiness punch list. It is a planning estimate at grid resolution, treating footprints (not clearance halos) as the obstacles, and it is not an egress-code analysis: it does not model door-swing blockage of a path, occupant load, travel distance, or exit counts.

### Dimension lines

Dimensions are permanent measured lines, placed with two clicks and stored on the scenario, distinct from the measuring tape, which is a transient overlay that leaves nothing behind. A dimension is drawn as a thin line between its two points with architectural slash ticks at each end and the distance labeled at the midpoint; the distance recomputes from the endpoints, so dragging either end (or the whole line) updates it live, and it is formatted in the current display unit like every other length. Dimensions render on screen and on the printed plat, and ride through export, import, and autosave with the rest of the document. They are annotations for communication, not geometry the checks read: TRIM and the utility engine do not consult them.

### Readiness punch list

The punch list is an aggregator, not a new check: it gathers the findings the tool already computes into one categorized list. TRIM contributes the clearance conflicts, machines extending past the walls, and door-swing and column interferences; the utility compute contributes unfed and wrong-voltage loads, overloaded and hot circuits, dust shortfalls, and air over-draw; and the panel schedule and service load contribute panel-space overflow and a service that cannot carry the demand. Each item carries a severity (error or warning) and a group (Clearances, Electrical, Panel, Dust, Air), and the list is sorted errors first, then by group. A live badge in the header summarizes the state (a check when clear, a count otherwise) so the plan can be watched toward green as issues are resolved. It prints as a check-off sheet. Because it only reflects what the other checks find, it stays correct for free as those checks evolve, and it inherits their caveats: it is a planning aid to clear before install, not a substitute for field verification or code review.

### Lighting coverage

The lighting estimate turns the placed fixtures into a foot-candle map. Each fixture's output is taken as its wattage times a fixed efficacy (110 lm/W), so a 40W LED reads about 4,400 lumens. The headline average is the lumen method: total lumens times a coefficient of utilization (0.6, a generic shop value) times a light-loss factor (0.8), divided by the floor area. The heatmap is a separate point-method calculation, each fixture treated as a downward point source and its contribution to each floor cell falling off with distance and mounting height, then the whole grid is scaled so its mean equals the lumen-method average. That keeps the headline number standard while showing honestly where the floor is bright and where it is dim. The average is compared to a woodworking target band (roughly 30 to 50 fc general, 75 to 100 at the bench), and the map reports the share of floor above each threshold. Mounting height is adjustable and changes the distribution (lower fixtures pool light more tightly) without changing the lumen-method average. It is a planning aid to catch a dark corner or an unlit bench, not a photometric layout, and it does not model reflector optics, surface reflectances, or daylight.

### Service load

The service load estimate sits under the panel schedule. It sums the connected load of every branch on the panel in volt-amps (each run's design load times its voltage), identifies the largest motor (a machine, collector, or compressor), and adds twenty-five percent of that largest motor to the total for a demand load, following the spirit of the NEC largest-motor rule. Dividing the demand by the service voltage gives the demand amperage, which is checked against the panel's main: if it fits, the verdict reports the headroom; if not, it reports the shortfall and suggests the next standard service size (100, 125, 150, 200, and up) that would carry it. It is a planning estimate to size a service and spot an obvious over-draw early, not a stamped load calculation, and it does not apply the full set of NEC demand factors for continuous loads, appliances, or dwelling-unit methods.

### Panel schedule

The panel schedule turns the assigned circuits into a breaker directory laid out like a real panel: positions numbered one upward, odd in the left column and even in the right, with each horizontal row sharing a leg and the legs alternating row to row (A, B, A, B), so a two-pole 240V breaker occupies two vertically adjacent slots in one column and lands on both legs. Single-pole 120V breakers are placed on whichever leg is currently lighter, which balances the panel. Each slot reports its circuit letter, breaker rating, a plain description of what it feeds (a machine's name, a count of outlets or fixtures, an outlet's rating), and its connected load against the breaker. The summary totals the connected load on each leg (a 240V load sits on both; a 120V load on its one leg) and reports the imbalance and the slots used against the panel's space count. It is a planning schedule, not a stamped panel directory, and prints as its own titled sheet.

### Materials takeoff

The takeoff turns the wired utilities into a shopping list for the active scenario. Outlets are counted by voltage and amperage. Breakers come from the panel circuits, grouped by rating and by pole (120V single, 240V double). Copper cable is grouped by conductor gauge, sized to each circuit's breaker on the NEC copper ampacity ladder (15A to 14 AWG, 20A to 12, 30A to 10, 40A to 8, 50A to 6, 60A to 4); the run length is the cable length, and a buy figure adds fifteen percent for slack and terminations, rounded up to the nearest five. Dust duct is grouped by diameter with a straight length, an elbow count from the interior vertices of each run, and one blast gate per branch. The compressed-air line is totalled in length with its elbow count. Fixed equipment (panels, collectors, compressors) is counted. Lengths render in the current display unit. The takeoff is a planning aid: it counts what the drawing shows and does not add couplings, boxes, connectors, or fasteners, which vary by method. It exports two ways: Copy list yields plain text, and Print / PDF lays out a titled letter sheet with a snapshot of the plat above the list, each line prefixed with a check box for use at the counter.

### Autosave and persistence

The model is still local-first with Export and Import as the canonical way to keep and move plans. On top of that, the tool autosaves the working plan to the browser's local storage after every change, debounced so a burst of edits writes once. The save reuses the same snapshot mechanism as undo/redo (the document minus the built-in library, which is reattached on load), and the header shows an honest state: Saving while a change settles, Saved with a timestamp once stored, and Autosave off where storage is unavailable (some embedded previews block it), in which case Export is the fallback. Autosave holds one working plan, is per-browser and per-device, and is restored automatically on load; it is a safety net against a lost tab, not a replacement for exported files.

### Compressed air

A machine with an `airPort` draws shop air at a stated demand (CFM) and working pressure (PSI). Wire all connects each air consumer to the nearest compressor node by an air run, which routes like a dust run (an automatic L, or manual bends you place and drag). The check is a capacity rollup, not yet a pressure-drop model: the summed demand of all air consumers is compared against the total rated delivery (SCFM) of the compressors, with a warning past eighty percent and an error over capacity, plus an error for any air consumer when no compressor is present. Pipe-diameter pressure drop over distance is a future refinement, so a long air line is not yet penalized the way a long duct run is.

### Service equipment power

A dust collector and an air compressor are motor loads, so each carries its own power spec (voltage, amps, dedicated) and Wire all pulls a dedicated circuit to it from the nearest panel, exactly as a large machine would. Defaults are 240V (a collector at 15A, a compressor at 20A) and can be edited per node; a collector or compressor placed without a panel to feed it, or needing 240V where only 120V is supplied, is flagged like any other unreachable load. Their circuits, breakers, and cable appear in the materials takeoff alongside the machines and outlets.

### Receptacles and NEC grouping

Outlets are placed as receptacle nodes around the room, each with a user-set voltage (120 or 240) and amperage rating. Wire all runs each outlet to the nearest panel and groups it onto a circuit the way an electrician would. A general 120V receptacle is treated as a 180VA load (NEC 220.14(I)); those pack onto a shared breaker of matching rating while the summed design load stays under eighty percent, which works out to about ten 20A outlets or eight 15A outlets per circuit. A 240V outlet, or any receptacle rated 30A or more, gets its own dedicated breaker sized to its rating, the way a range or welder receptacle would. Machine loads and receptacle loads never share a circuit. The panel main check uses these design loads, not the receptacle nameplate ratings, so a wall of 20A outlets does not read as hundreds of connected amps.

### Electrical run encoding

Electrical runs on the plat are drawn to be read at a glance. Color carries voltage: one hue for 120V, a distinct one for 240V. The dash pattern carries amperage, growing coarser with the breaker: a fine dash for 15A, medium for 20A, long for 30A, and a dash-dot for 50A and up. Each run is labeled at its midpoint with the letter of the circuit it belongs to, so two runs sharing a breaker read as the same circuit.

### Utility visibility

The three utility systems (dust, electrical, air) each have a show/hide toggle, with show-all and hide-all shortcuts. Hidden runs are also skipped by selection, so a hidden line cannot be picked or edited. Visibility is a view setting; it does not change the graph or any of the rollups, which always account for every run.

The dust-run budget (effective length versus a per-diameter ceiling) is a quick screen. The primary check is a static-pressure model. The collector carries a linear fan curve running from its rated free-air airflow at zero static pressure down to its shutoff static pressure at zero airflow. Each branch imposes a system curve, static pressure rising with airflow through the branch's diameter and effective length (straight run plus elbow equivalents), using a Darcy-style friction fit for smooth metal duct. The operating airflow is where the two curves cross, found by bisection. That delivered airflow is compared against a capture target, the airflow that sustains roughly a four-thousand foot-per-minute branch velocity, and a branch that delivers less than its target is a warning. The model omits hood entry losses and treats duct as smooth metal, so it is a design aid rather than a certified calculation.

## 7. PLAT title-block fields

This section pins the fields on an issued plat, closing the last open item from section 13 of the data-model spec. The printable sheet renders on a white ground regardless of the on-screen theme, and carries a title block, a scale bar, a north arrow, and a legend.

Title-block fields:

- **Project**: the document title (`meta.title`).
- **Facility**: the room name (`facility.name`).
- **Scale**: the drawing scale, presently one inch to one inch (the plat is drawn true to size in inches).
- **Revision**: `meta.revision`, defaulting to A.
- **Date**: `meta.date`, stamped with the current date at print time if unset.
- **Drawn**: `meta.author`, defaulting to M.B. Parks.
- **Sheet**: the scenario name being issued.
- **Instrument mark**: BAYPLAN FI-075.

Supporting marks: a north arrow keyed to plan north, a scale bar showing a known two-foot length for reference when the sheet is resized on paper, and a legend covering the hard, soft, and shared clearances plus the dust and electrical run styles.
