# BAYPLAN (FI-075): ENVELOPE Conventions and Built-in Library

Companion to `bayplan-data-model.md` (section 6, ClearanceZone) and `bayplan-seed-library.json`.
This document pins the default clearance depths per machine category, closing the ENVELOPE open item from section 13 of the data-model spec.

Document revision: 1.0.0
Schema version: 1.0.0

---

## 1. What this covers

The data-model spec defines *how* a clearance zone is shaped (rect, polygon, arc) and *how* conflicts resolve (hard, soft, shared). It deliberately left the actual default numbers to ENVELOPE. This document supplies them: a local-frame convention every built-in definition follows, a rationale table for the default depths, and per-category notes. The machine-readable output is `bayplan-seed-library.json`, generated by `build_seed.py`.

These are defaults meant to be tuned. The whole point of seed-then-own is that you measure your real planer and nudge it. The numbers below are defensible starting values, not gospel.

## 2. Local frame convention

Every `MachineDef` footprint and clearance is expressed in the machine's own local frame, so it rotates and mirrors with the placement.

| Element | Convention |
|---------|-----------|
| Origin (0,0) | The anchor (blade, spindle, cutterhead, table center) |
| +y | Back, away from the operator |
| -y | Front, the operator side |
| +x | Operator's right |
| -x | Operator's left |
| Units | Inches, floating point |

### Footprint edge names

Built-in footprints are axis-aligned rectangles. A `RectOffEdge` clearance references one of four named edges, and the strip projects outward from that edge:

- **front** = the minimum-y edge (operator side)
- **back** = the maximum-y edge
- **left** = the minimum-x edge
- **right** = the maximum-x edge

The anchor is not always the centroid. A table saw's anchor is the blade, so its footprint is offset (blade 14 inches from the left edge, 13 from the front). The edge names still refer to the footprint's real sides regardless of where the anchor sits inside it.

### Arc angle convention

Arc clearances use degrees clockwise from +y (back), matching the plat's clockwise-from-north scheme with +y as local north:

- 0 = back (+y)
- 90 = right (+x)
- 180 = front (-y)
- 270 = left (-x)

The wood lathe's outboard swing, for example, is an arc centered at the headstock spindle sweeping from 180 to 270 (front to left), the quadrant where an outboard bowl blank rotates.

### minHeight

`minHeight` is the height a zone needs to be clear up to. A value of `null` means full height (floor to head). A numeric value means the zone only needs clearing to that height, so a low cabinet or a neighbor's base can sit under a feed lane but never under an operator zone. This is what lets a table saw's outfeed lane pass over a short storage unit while an operator stance cannot.

## 3. Default clearance depths (the rationale table)

| Zone kind | Default depth | Severity | minHeight | Reasoning |
|-----------|--------------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Feed lane, sheet-goods capable | 96 | shared | deck + stock | 8 feet each side to rip or crosscut a full sheet |
| Feed lane, long stock | 72 to 96 | shared | deck + stock | Matched to typical board and trim lengths |
| Operator standing | 30 (36 comfortable) | soft | null | Room to stand and work; full height |
| Operator seated | 30 | soft | null | Chair plus reach at a bench |
| Service access | 18 to 24 | soft | null | Panels, bins, bit changes, wiring |
| Spark and fire standoff | 36 to 48 | hard | null | Welding slag and grinder spark cones |
| Immediate blade danger | 8 | hard | null | The kill zone right at the cutter |
| Swing (lathe outboard) | radius + margin | hard | null | Nothing may enter the arc of a spinning blank |
| End clearance (lathe, bar stock) | 18 to 24 | hard or soft | varies | Bar through the spindle bore, long stock past the tailstock |

### Why feed lanes are shared, not hard

A table saw's outfeed genuinely wants to overlap the jointer's operator walk space, or an aisle, or another machine's low base. Marking feed lanes `shared` lets two feed zones (or a feed zone and an aisle) coexist without a false conflict, while a `shared` lane hitting a `hard` zone (someone's blade danger zone, a lathe swing) still errors. The `minHeight` on a feed lane reinforces this: the lane only needs clearing to stock height, so it can pass over low obstructions.

### Why operator zones are soft

Two operator stances that will not be used at the same time may overlap; that is a warning, not an error. You know your own workflow. The tool flags the overlap so you make the call deliberately rather than discovering it at the bench.

### Why fire, blade, and swing are hard

These are safety zones, not convenience zones. A combustible inside a welding spark spray, a body inside a blade path, or a fixture inside a lathe swing is an error the tool should refuse to pass quietly. Hard is the only correct severity for anything that can hurt you.

### Primitives exercised by the seed

The seed is also a coverage test for the geometry engine. Across 50 clearance zones it exercises every primitive and every severity:

- **rect** on all four named edges, with and without `minHeight`
- **arc** on the wood lathe (the outboard swing), the one primitive hardest to get right under mirroring
- **shared** severity on feed lanes and the assembly apron (15 zones)
- **hard** severity on blade, fire, swing, and bar-stock zones (7 zones)
- **soft** severity on operator and service zones (the balance)

If a rendering or conflict-checking change passes against this seed, it has seen every shape and severity combination the model defines.

## 5. Category defaults not yet in the seed

Categories a future seed pass could add, with the depth family they would inherit:

- Belt or disc sander: operator soft, dust 4in, no long feed lane.
- Router table: feed lanes like a jointer, dust 2.5in at the fence.
- Surface grinder or metal cutoff saw: long feed lanes, coolant or chip service.
- Spray or finishing booth: fire standoff (hard), strong ventilation clearance.
- Sheet-goods rack and lumber rack: no operator zone, a loading apron (shared).

Each would follow the section-3 rationale table rather than inventing new depths.

## 6. SERVICES defaults (duct and electrical)

This section pins the tunable defaults the SERVICES station uses, closing the elbow-equivalent open item from section 13 of the data-model spec. Like the clearance depths, these are defensible starting values, not gospel, and they live in the `Services` module of `bayplan.html`.

### Duct elbow equivalents

A fitting adds resistance equivalent to some length of straight duct. BAYPLAN charges each bend in a run an equivalent length, in feet, for one ninety-degree elbow at that diameter, and scales linearly for shallower bends (a forty-five-degree bend costs half a ninety).

| Duct diameter | 90-degree elbow equivalent |
|---------------|----------------------------|
| 2.5 in | 4 ft |
| 4 in | 6 ft |
| 5 in | 7 ft |
| 6 in | 9 ft |

Elbows are inferred from a run's polyline: at each interior vertex the deflection from straight is measured, and the elbow charge is that table value times (deflection / 90). A straight run has no interior vertices and therefore no elbow charge. Auto-routed dust runs turn one orthogonal corner, so they carry exactly one ninety-degree elbow unless the collector and the machine port already share an axis.

### Duct effective-length budget

Effective length is straight run plus elbow equivalents. A run past the recommended maximum for its diameter earns a warning, on the principle that a long small-diameter run cannot keep chips moving.

| Duct diameter | Recommended max effective length |
|---------------|----------------------------------|
| 2.5 in | 20 ft |
| 4 in | 35 ft |
| 5 in | 45 ft |
| 6 in | 55 ft |

These are rule-of-thumb ceilings for typical single-stage and cyclone collectors in a small shop, deliberately conservative. A precise design would compute static pressure against the collector's fan curve; that refinement is noted for a future pass.

### Electrical rollup

Each machine carries a `PowerReq` (voltage, amps, phase, dedicated). When a panel exists, SERVICES rolls up connected load into a 120V bucket and a 240V bucket. Two checks apply: a machine that needs 240V when no panel supplies it is an error, and connected load past eighty percent of the largest panel's main is a warning. For the load-versus-main comparison, 120V loads are counted at half their amperage, since two 120V circuits share a 240V main. Panel defaults are a 240V service at 100A main with 20 spaces; these are editable per node.
