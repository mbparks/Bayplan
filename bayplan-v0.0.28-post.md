A plan without dimensions is half a plan. You can lay out every machine perfectly, and the moment you print it and carry it to the shop, the first thing you want to know is the number: how wide is that aisle, how far is the bench from the wall, is there really four feet to feed a sheet through. The measuring tape has been in the tool for a while, but a tape is a thing you hold up and then let go. What was missing was the mark that stays. Now it is here.

## The mark that stays

There is a Dim button now. Click it, click a point, click another point, and a dimension line snaps in between them, drawn the way a drafter draws one, a clean line with little slash ticks at each end and the distance called out right in the middle. Forty-eight inches. Thirty-six to the wall. Keep clicking and you keep adding them, so you can dimension the whole layout in one pass, the aisles, the setbacks, the reach behind the jointer.

And then they stay. That is the whole point, and the difference from the tape. The tape tells you a number and vanishes. A dimension is part of the drawing. It saves with the plan, it comes back when you open it, it prints on the sheet you carry out to the shop. When someone looks at your plat, the critical clearances are right there, called out, not something they have to go measure themselves and hope they got the scale right.

## Living numbers

The nice touch is that the number is alive. Grab either end of a dimension and drag it, and the distance updates as you go, because it is not a label you typed, it is the actual measured length between two points you control. Move the bench, drag the dimension's end to follow, and it reads the new gap. Grab the middle and slide the whole thing somewhere clearer. And because it is a length like any other in the tool, it speaks whatever units you have it set to, feet and inches or meters and centimeters, and switches when you switch.

I kept it honest about what it is, too. A dimension is for communicating, a note to whoever reads the drawing, including future you. It is not geometry the tool's checks read; TRIM still does its own clearance math on the real footprints, and the dimension you drew does not change that. It is the human layer on top, the callouts that turn a layout into a drawing someone can build from.

Three new checks, plus five in the harness: that a dimension survives an export and an import, that it draws on the printed plat, and that it comes back through autosave. A hundred and sixty-five checks now, green, and the drag fix that all of this leans on still holds.

Lay it out, then dimension it. That is how a drawing gets made, and now the tool does both.
